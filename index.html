<h1 align="center"> SENECA </h1>

<p><b>Lucius Annaeus Seneca</b> (c. 4 BCE – 65 CE), often simply called <b>Seneca</b>,</p>

<p align="center"> Key facts about Seneca: </p>

<h2> 1. Life and Background </h2>

<ol>

<li> Born in Corduba (modern-day Córdoba, Spain) into a wealthy equestrian family of Romanized Iberian origin.</li>

<li> His father, Seneca the Elder, was a well-known rhetorician </li>

<li>Moved to Rome as a young man to study rhetoric and philosophy.</li>

<li>Suffered from poor health throughout his life, possibly tuberculosis.</li>

</ol>

<h3> 2. Political Career </h3>

<ol>

<li> Rose to prominence under Emperor Claudius, though he was exiled to Corsica in 41 CE on charges of adultery with Julia Livilla (Claudius’s niece). </li>

<li> Recalled in 49 CE by Agrippina the Younger to tutor her son, Nero. </li>

<li> Became Nero’s chief advisor after the emperor’s accession in 54 CE, alongside the praetorian prefect Burrus. </li>

<li> Accumulated great wealth and influence, which later drew criticism for inconsistency with his Stoic teachings. </li>

</ol>

<h3> 3. Philosophy </h3>

<ul>

<li> A leading exponent of Stoicism, emphasizing: </li>

<li> Virtue as the highest good. </li>

<li> Reason as the guiding force of life. </li>

<li> Acceptance of fate (amor fati). </li>

<li> Inner tranquillity (ataraxia) through detachment from external goods. </li>

</ul>

<ul>

<li> Wrote extensively in the form of letters, essays, and dialogues. </li>

<li> Key works include: </li>

<li> <i>Letters to Lucilius</i> — letters offering practical Stoic advice. </li>

<li> <i>On the Shortness of Life</i> — on using time wisely. </li>

<li> <i>On Anger</i> — a psychological and philosophical analysis of managing emotions. </li>

<li> <i>On the Happy Life</i> — defining true happiness through virtue. </li>

</ul>

<h3> 4. Tragedies </h3>

<ul>

<li> Also known for his tragic plays, such as <i>Medea</i>, <i>Phaedra</i>, <i>Thyestes</i>, and <i>Oedipus</i>. </li>

<li> These works are notable for their rhetorical intensity, exploration of passion and madness, and influence on Renaissance drama (especially Elizabethan tragedy). </li>

</ul>

<h3> 5. Death </h3>

<ul>

<li> Fell out of favor with Nero around 62–65 CE. </li>

<li> Accused (likely falsely) of involvement in the Pisonian conspiracy to assassinate Nero. </li>

<li> Ordered to commit suicide in 65 CE. </li>

</ul>
